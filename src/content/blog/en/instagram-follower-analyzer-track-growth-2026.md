## How to Spot Fake Followers — 5 Red Flags

Not every suspicious account is a bot, but there are clear patterns that indicate purchased followers.

### 1. Sudden Follower Spikes

Real growth is gradual. If an account gains 50,000 followers overnight without going viral, those followers were likely purchased.

### 2. Extremely Low Engagement Rate

An account with 100,000 followers should average at least 1,000 to 3,000 likes per post. If they consistently get under 200, the audience is mostly inactive or fake.

### 3. Generic or Empty Profiles in the Follower List

Scroll through the followers and look for profiles with no posts, default avatars, and random username strings like "user38291847." These are hallmarks of bot accounts.

### 4. Follower-to-Following Ratio Issues

Accounts that follow 7,000 people but have 500,000 followers often used follow-for-follow services or bot farms.

### 5. Comments That Don't Match the Content

Generic comments like "Great post!" or emoji-only responses from accounts with no real activity are signs of engagement pods or bots.

### What percentage of fake followers is considered normal?

Most legitimate accounts have between 5% and 15% inactive or suspicious followers. This is normal because some users abandon accounts over time. Anything above 25% is a red flag that the account may have purchased followers.

### How often should I check an account's follower growth?

For ongoing monitoring, checking weekly or bi-weekly gives you a good picture of growth trends. For one-time audits, such as before an influencer partnership, a single comprehensive analysis is usually sufficient.

### Can follower analyzers detect engagement pods?

Engagement pods — groups of users who agree to like and comment on each other's posts — are harder to detect than bot followers. However, analyzers can flag suspicious patterns like identical comment timing and repetitive interactions from the same small group of accounts.
